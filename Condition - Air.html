Welcome & Consent

Thank you for agreeing to participate in this research study. Please read the following information carefully before proceeding.

About the study

This study is conducted by Filip Olsson at the European University Institute (EUI). It examines how media coverage of climate change influences public attitudes, behavioural intentions, and policy preferences. The study is funded by the Magnus Bergvall Foundation.

What you will do

You will read a short news article and then answer a series of questions about your views on climate change. The survey takes approximately 8 to 12 minutes to complete.

Your rights

  • Participation is voluntary. You may withdraw at any time without penalty.
  • Your responses are anonymous. Individual data cannot be linked to you after collection.
  • You may withdraw your data within 30 days by emailing filip.olsson@eui.eu with your Prolific ID. After 30 days the dataset is fully anonymised and individual responses can no longer be located.

The Air Americans Grew Up With

By Sarah Chen · Published May 12, 2025 · 3 min read

For much of the twentieth century, poor air quality was a routine feature of American life. In cities and towns across the country, the air carried high concentrations of pollutants from coal-burning power plants, steel mills, leaded gasoline, and rapid expansion of car ownership with little oversight of vehicle emissions. Few gave it much thought, partly because the worst of it was not always something you could see or smell.

By the time most of today's adults were children, ground-level ozone and particulate matter had become persistent problems in American metropolitan areas. Monitoring data from those decades shows consistently high readings across a broad range of cities, not just industrial centers like Pittsburgh and Detroit, but also mid-sized cities and suburban areas downwind of highways and manufacturing facilities, where pollution accumulated in the absence of stronger controls.

"It was just the air," said Dr. Michelle Park, an environmental researcher at the University of Michigan who grew up in the Chicago suburbs. "You didn't think of it as a crisis. Occasionally you'd notice a smell, or a haze on a hot day. But mostly it was invisible, and the readings were still poor."

American Air Today

By Sarah Chen · Published May 19, 2025 · 2 min read

The picture has changed considerably since the decades when today's adults were growing up. The cheap coal that powered industry has been gradually displaced by cleaner alternatives. Cars and trucks went through successive generations of cleaner engines, older vehicles came off the roads, and the buses that ran on the dirtiest fuel were replaced.

When researchers measure pollution at the same monitoring stations that recorded the worst years, the readings have dropped sharply. Levels of fine particulate pollution have fallen by more than forty percent compared with earlier decades. Levels of the smog-forming chemicals that used to discolor summer skies have dropped by similar amounts.

"I think people forget how bad it actually was," said Dr. Elena Hartmann, an epidemiologist who has studied air pollution since the early 1990s. "The improvements have been larger than most people realize."

Public health departments now treat these changes as among the most significant environmental successes of recent decades.

Health & Environment

American Air Today

By Sarah Chen · Published May 19, 2025 · 2 min read

The picture has not changed much since the decades when today's adults were growing up. Coal is still being burned, diesel trucks still idle along freight corridors, and the same factories operating in earlier decades are largely still operating. On top of that, wildfire smoke now drifts across large parts of the country every summer, adding a source of pollution that barely existed a generation ago.

When researchers measure pollution at the same monitoring stations that recorded the worst years, the readings have barely moved. Levels of fine particulate pollution have dropped by only a few percent compared with earlier decades. Levels of smog-forming chemicals have changed by similar amounts.

"What strikes me is how little has actually moved," said Dr. Marcus Olsen, a public health researcher who has tracked air quality for more than twenty years. "We talk about pollution as a problem of the past. The numbers tell a different story."

Public health departments now treat these numbers as among the most stubborn failures of recent decades.

Debrief

Thank you for completing this survey.

This study examines how different framings of climate change in the media influence people's attitudes, behavioural intentions, and policy preferences.

The article you read was one of several constructed narratives that participants were randomly assigned to. The articles were written for the purposes of this research. The factual claims within each article are grounded in published scientific literature, but the framing and emphasis were deliberately varied across conditions.
